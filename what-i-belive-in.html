I have incorporated many of the aspects of Greek and Roman mythology into my life. For example in Greek mythology there are three Fates who are personified as three very old women who spin the threads of human destiny. They control how a person’s life begins, progresses and ends. Another aspect of Greek mythology that I believe in is the Underworld in Greek Mythology, controlled by Hades; the god of the underworld. The underworld is where people go after death. In the Greek underworld, the souls of the dead still existed, but they were insubstantial, and flitted around the underworld with no sense of purpose. The dead within the Homeric underworld lack menos, or strength, and therefore they cannot influence those on earth. They also lack phrenes, or wit, and are heedless of what goes on around them and on the earth above them. Their lives in the underworld were very neutral, so all social statuses and political positions were eliminated and no one was able to use their previous lives to their advantage in the underworld. The Elysian Fields (also referred to as the Isles of the Blessed) was a utopian, paradisiacal afterlife reserved for specially distinguished individuals. Eventually, as concepts of the afterlife broadened and became more 'democratic', the generally righteous could be sent to the Elysian Fields after being judged by the underworld judges, Rhadamanthus and Minos. This was the Greeks equivalent of the afterlife/reincarnation.

I've taken a lot away from roman and greek mythology, I believe in both of their pantheons of gods/goddesses. I've also taken aspects from Hinduism, like reincarnation, and good and bad karma. Another religion that I’ve taken aspects from is Buddhism, one of the aspect from buddhism that I strongly agree with is: Anicca which is the process of the soul, constantly mutating through each re-incarnation, I find this aspect very interesting because while Greek and Roman mythology include an afterlife, they never explain what happens after, and I feel that this definition is what is missing from what Greek and Roman mythology define as the afterlife.
